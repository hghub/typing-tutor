## The New Solar Question in Pakistan

Under older solar advice, bigger was often treated as better. Install as much capacity as your roof could take, export the surplus, and let net metering do the rest. That logic is weaker now.

With lower export value under the current net billing environment, the smarter question is often: **should you install a smaller system that mostly serves your own daytime load?**

## When a Smaller System Can Be Better

A smaller self-consumption-focused setup often wins when:

- your daytime home usage is limited
- most family members are out during solar hours
- your DISCO meter and application fee is expensive
- your monthly bill is moderate, not very high
- you want a lower upfront investment and faster payback

## Why Net Billing Changes the Math

If exported units earn far less than the units you avoid buying from the grid, then oversized systems lose some of their appeal. The best solar unit is often the one you consume yourself, not the one you export.

That means a household with lower daytime use may be financially better off with a smaller system and stronger self-use than with a larger system that creates more exported energy but also requires more upfront cash and a separate meter process.

## The Upfront-Cost Difference Matters

The full-system decision is not only about monthly savings. It is also about:

- extra panel cost
- inverter sizing
- net billing application cost
- meter fee
- capital tied up on day one

If a smaller system reduces your upfront spend materially, the lower initial investment can sometimes outweigh the difference in monthly savings.

## Who Should Usually Still Go Bigger?

A fuller system still makes sense when:

- your bill is high enough that the savings pool is large
- you already use a good share of electricity during the day
- you can justify the net billing fee quickly
- you expect tariffs to keep rising sharply
- your home has strong, consistent solar usage across the year
